Krishna's Birth Story

Lord Krishna's birth took place in the prison of his maternal uncle. Devaki, the sister of King Kansa, was married to Vasudeva, a prominent leader of the Yadav dynasty. Once, when Vasudeva was taking his sister to her in-laws, there was a divine proclamation that the eighth child of Devaki and Vasudeva would be the cause of Kansa's death. Alarmed by this revelation, Kansa imprisoned his sister and her husband.


Subsequently, Devaki and Vasudeva had seven children, all of whom were slain by Kansa. When the eighth child, Krishna, was about to be born, Kansa took special precautions. However, the divine play of the Lord prevailed, and all the guards fell asleep. The prison doors opened on their own at the time when Krishna was born during a torrential downpour and complete darkness.


Following the divine instructions, Vasudeva carried baby Krishna to Gokul, where Nand Baba's wife, Yashoda, had given birth to a baby girl. Vasudeva exchanged Krishna with the baby girl, and the prison doors closed again as he returned. The guards woke up, and the news reached Kansa that the eighth child who would be his downfall had been born. Despite Kansa's efforts, the divine play of the Lord continued, and Krishna grew up in Gokul under the care of Nand Baba and Yashoda..

When Vasudeva returned to the prison with the baby girl, Kansa became aware that Devaki's eighth child had been born. He promptly went to the prison and moved to harm the baby girl. At that poignant moment, Mother Devaki stepped forward, restraining him with dignity, and spoke, "O King, the cause of your destiny is my son, not this innocent girl. I have been blessed with a daughter. Spare her, for she is my only hope now."


Despite her heartfelt plea, Kansa remained unmoved and callously proceeded to harm the innocent child. He heartlessly threw the infant to the ground. At that very instant, Mother Bhavani manifested herself, and with divine grace, she proclaimed, "Oh misguided one, the one fated to bring about the end of your life has already taken birth. Narayan has incarnated."


This sacred intervention marked the commencement of Lord Krishna's divine journey, and Kansa's misguided actions only played a role in the unfolding of the divine play of the Lord.

Krishna's Childhood Stories

"Krishna's Heavenly Revelation"

One day, Krishna and His elder brother Balaram were collecting fruits and jamuns in the garden. As Krishna was still a small child and couldn't reach the trees, He picked up some mud from the ground and put it in His mouth. Other children witnessed this and complained to their mothers. Yashoda rushed to them and asked Krishna if He had eaten mud. He gestured a negative response by shaking His head. Yashoda then asked Krishna to open His mouth, but He was hesitant and refused to comply. However, when Yashoda looked at Him sternly, He reluctantly opened His mouth. To her astonishment, instead of mud, Yashoda saw the entire cosmos inside Krishna's mouth – stars, skies, mountains, and vast oceans. She was filled with wonder, and Krishna continued to smile. At that moment, Yashoda realized that her son was not an ordinary child but the divine incarnate Himself.

"Krishna's Butter Adventure"

Once, when Yashoda was not around, Krishna called his friends. They climbed on top of each other, successfully reaching the pots of butter. Together, they lowered a pot filled with butter and joyfully shared and ate it. Unfortunately, they were unaware that Yashoda had returned. While Krishna's friends hurriedly went back to their homes, Krishna faced a scolding for his mischievous behavior. she decided to put an end to Krishna's mischief by tying Him up. Yashoda brought a rope but realized it was too short. She then fetched a longer one, but it too fell short. After trying a third rope, which also proved insufficient, Yashoda came to the realization that her son was extraordinary. However, following this realization, Lord Krishna allowed His mother to tie Him up, bringing joy to her heart.

story of Govardhan Parvat

The story of Govardhan Parvat is familiar to everyone. It is one of the well-known divine plays of Lord Krishna. In essence, Lord Krishna, unknown to the villagers about His divine nature, was angered by Indra Dev and, in response, caused heavy rainfall in the village. To protect the villagers, Shri Krishna lifted the Govardhan Parvat on His little finger and provided shelter to all Mathuravasis beneath it. For seven days, without consuming anything, Shri Krishna stood holding the Govardhan Parvat. On the eighth day, when the rain ceased, the villagers were allowed to go out

"Krishna's Divine Test"

Once, Lord Brahma decided to test Krishna's divinity. He instructed all of Krishna's friends to take the form of cows and hide in a cave. When Krishna couldn't find any of His friends, He manifested avatars of all His companions and returned to Vrindavan. Disturbed and worried, Lord Brahma came to Vrindavan and, upon witnessing Krishna in all forms, realized that he shouldn't have tested Krishna's divine nature.

"Krishna's Divine Encounter with Malin"

Malin, a vendor from Mathura, comes to Gokul to sell vegetables and fruits. One day, as she shouts to sell fruits, young Krishna hears her and runs out to buy them. Krishna, adorned with divine attributes, runs towards Malin with love. In the exchange, Krishna spills some grains, and Malin, overwhelmed, sees Krishna as a divine presence. She wishes Krishna to come into her lap, and Krishna, understanding her feelings, sits in her lap. This moment deepens Malin's maternal love for Krishna.


Krishna, recognizing Malin's pure love, fills her hands with fruits as a gesture of love. Malin, filled with joy, runs home with Krishna's touch and image imprinted in her heart. Krishna's divine presence has transformed her, and she no longer desires to return home. Instead, she carries the basket with Krishna's memory to her house, where she discovers Krishna has filled it with precious jewels.


The divine light from the jewels illuminates Malin's surroundings, revealing only Krishna in that divine radiance. Malin willingly surrenders everything to Krishna, and her desire to return home fades away. She enters her house with the basket, now filled with jewels, and her life becomes blessed by Krishna's divine presence. Krishna embraces her as his own, and Malin becomes a devoted follower of Lord Krishna.

Radhe krishna and Raas Leela

Radha-Krishna's love story began at a very young age. According to tradition, when Krishna was a young boy, he started playing the flute. Whenever Krishna played the flute, everyone around entered a state of trance that was entirely pure and beautiful. Even the gopis who were working would be captivated, and Krishna's divine melody would bring them into a dance of love and devotion. Among them, one gopi, Radha, she always felt a deep connection with him. She lost herself in the beauty of Krishna's music and his divine presence. The entire cosmos longed for Krishna, but he, in turn, yearned for Radha. Their love story blossomed in the gardens, and they danced together in the enchanting groves known as Nidhivan(Madhuban) in Vrindavan. Even today also Raas leela takes place in Nidhivan in mid-night and nobody is allowed to go there at night.

The meeting of Radha and Krishna in the gardens, their playful dances, and the celebration of festivals with friends and relatives are eternally remembered in Vrindavan. The place is known as the abode of Radha-Krishna's love, and devotees still visit to feel the love and devotion that permeates the air in this sacred spot.

The love between them is impossible to describein words. Radha-Krishna's love story remains incomplete without the Bansuri. Everyone enjoys the divine leelas of Lord Krishna, but few knowthat Krishna played the bansuri only for Radha.When they felt that the time had come for them to part ways, Krishna wanted to give Radha a gift. However, she refused to take anything. In the end, Krishna decided to play a piece of divine music for her. This was the last performance of his flute, and after this, he never played it again.

Their love was pure and filled with devotion, transcending the ordinary bonds of man and woman. Radha's devotion to Krishna was profound. After many years of separation, Radha decided to see Krishna one last time. At that time, she was weak and old but still beautiful. She determined to cover a long distance to reach Krishna. By that time, she had freed herself from all domestic and familial responsibilities. Upon entering Krishna's palace, she caught a glimpse of Devakinandan and was amazed by the divine beauty of the Lord. In his last moments, Krishna asked her to make a wish, and she requested Krishna to play the bansuri for her during the day and night. She became absorbed in Krishna's spiritual form through the divine music of the bansuri. Listening to the devotional melody, she relinquished her body and left that place. Unable to bear Radha's death, Krishna, as a symbolic end to their love, broke his bansuri.

Radha and Krishna are globally renowned for their selfless love and devotion to each other. Though they loved each other selflessly, they never married. They sacrificed their love and separated, but in their hearts, they were always one. That's why Lord Krishna promised Radha that whenever people call upon him, they would have to call Radha first. According to a mythological belief, Radha represents his internal energy—his power on Earth. She always believed in the internal energy of Lord Krishna.

Consorts of Krishna

Lord Krishna's 16,108 marriages are intertwined with various tales, showcasing the diverse dimensions of his divine play and illustrating the profound devotion of his devotees.According to ancient Hindu scriptures, Lord Krishna had eight principal queens. Their names, in sequence, were Rukmani, Jambavanti, Satyabhama, Kalindi, Mitravinda, Satya, Bhadra, and Lakshmana.

The Marriage of 16,100 Maidens:

According to mythological stories, once the demon Narakasura imprisoned 16,000 maidens with the intention of offering them as a sacrifice. Lord Krishna defeated Narakasura, securing the freedom of these maidens. Upon returning home, the families of these maidens were hesitant to accept them due to societal norms and taboos associated with being captives. In response, Lord Krishna manifested in 16,000 different forms and married each maiden simultaneously. According to some traditions, when the families refused to accept these maidens due to societal shame, the maidens silently accepted Lord Krishna as their husband and began worshipping him with unwavering devotion.

Krishna in Mahabharata

"The profound teachings of Shri Krishna in the Bhagavad Gita and his role in the Mahabharata convey timeless lessons for humanity. In the Gita's 18th chapter, Krishna emphasizes the essence of the Karma theory and Dharma, urging individuals to contemplate their actions and make choices, for one's destiny is a consequence of personal decisions. He asserts that guidance is offered, but the responsibility lies with the individual. Despite being God, Krishna does not impose control over human actions or destinies. In the Mahabharata, Krishna attempts to avert war through diplomacy, illustrating the importance of peaceful resolution.

Krishna's role is that of a guide and philosopher, urging Arjuna to seek glory and fame through righteous deeds rather than fighting on his behalf. The narrative emphasizes the notion that individuals must face the consequences of their actions, and Krishna's role is not to dictate but to illuminate the path of righteousness.The complexity of Krishna's character invites contemplation and understanding, emphasizing the importance of seeking wisdom to comprehend the divine. The overarching lesson is that individuals have the freedom to choose their paths and must navigate life's complexities with discernment, guided by principles of righteousness and responsibility.

Teachings of Krishna

Describing Krishna's divine play, his glory, and beauty in a single page and a few words is indeed a challenging task. Just as humans can never become the Supreme Being, praising Krishna and extolling His virtues may tire us, but the glory of His leelas (divine activities) remains inexhaustible. Krishna is the epitome of beauty, the sovereign ruler, and the ultimate Brahman. His leelas are beyond measure. While we occasionally read about the Lord's stories from various sources and encounter different interpretations, the essence of His beauty and divine qualities remains consistent across all accounts. Whether in scriptures or various books, the descriptions of Krishna's beauty, His name, and His glory are synonymous. In every word, Krishna is beautiful, and that word becomes exquisite when infused with His name. The radiance of His name and the mention of Radha evoke a sublime beauty in every expression

Krishna is complete; He can assume multiple avatars, ranging from a beggar to a king, from an animal to various objects. In the company of 16,108 queens, Krishna manifested Himself in 16,108 forms, and Radha, distinct from all, asserts that she is Krishna Himself.Krishna always stands by His devotees and some people accuse Him of favoritism. However, they fail to realize that where there is a heart filled with love for Krishna, there is no unrighteousness; in other words, Krishna consistently takes the side of righteousness. He is the embodiment of dharma. Krishna even lets go of His devotees when their devotion is accompanied by arrogance. Moreover, Krishna never shows partiality; He remains impartial.
